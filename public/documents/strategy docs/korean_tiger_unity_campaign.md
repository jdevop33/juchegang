## Part 1: The Cultural Foundation

### 1.1 The Peninsula IS a Tiger

**Historical Origin**: In 1908, Korean intellectual Ch'oe Nams≈èn proposed that Korea resembles a tiger "with its paw raised, clawing as it runs towards the Asian continent." This was a direct counter-narrative to Japanese colonizers who depicted Korea as a weak rabbit.

**The Resistance Symbol**: During Japanese occupation, depicting Korea as a tiger became an act of defiance. The tiger represented:
- Strength vs. colonial weakness narratives
- Korean national consciousness
- Will to resist and reclaim territory

**Modern Usage**:
- 1988 Seoul Olympics: Hodori (orange Amur tiger mascot)
- 2018 PyeongChang Winter Olympics: Soohorang (white tiger, sacred guardian)
- DPRK has issued postage stamps showing the peninsula as a tiger
- Korean national soccer team uniforms feature tiger imagery

**Key Insight**: BOTH Koreas claim the tiger. It's one of the few symbols that transcends the division.

### 1.2 Tiger in Korean Mythology & Daily Life

**Dangun Creation Myth**:
The founding story of Korea features a tiger and a bear competing to become human. While the bear succeeded and became the ancestor of Koreans, the tiger is woven into the origin story itself. Both North and South claim Dangun as their founding myth.

**Sansin (Mountain Spirit)**:
In Korean shamanism, tigers are guardians of mountains‚Äîand Korea is 70% mountains. The tiger isn't just an animal; it's a spiritual protector of the land.

**Proverbs & Daily Life**:
40% of Korean animal folk tales feature tigers. Common proverbs include:
- "Even if you go into a tiger's den, if you stay alert, you'll live"
- "A tiger leaves his skin behind him while a man leaves his name behind"
- "Even a tiger comes when someone is talking about a tiger" (speak of the devil)

**Amur/Siberian Tiger**:
The species native to the Korean peninsula. Its historical range spans the ENTIRE peninsula‚Äînorth to south. The tiger doesn't recognize the 38th parallel.

**The Data (2024 KINU Survey)**:
- Only 52.9% of South Koreans believe unification is "necessary" (lowest in a decade)
- War generation: 73.6% support reunification
- Those born after 1991: LOWEST support of any age group

- Youth respond to CULTURAL content, not political messaging
- 67.8% of Korean YOUTH (elementary through high school) still think reunification is necessary
- The frame matters more than the facts
